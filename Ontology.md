# ONTOLOGICAL MAPPING: FUNCTIONAL DEFINITIONS ACROSS FRAMEWORKS

## Purpose
Document how key concepts carry different meanings/hidden variables depending on framework, preventing miscommunication and conceptual flattening.

---

## CONTROL

### Western Framework Definition
- Ability to dictate outcomes through force/regulation
- Centralized authority over processes
- Minimizing unpredictability through standardization
- **Hidden assumption**: Control requires domination

### Relational/Indigenous Framework Definition  
- Understanding field dynamics well enough to work with them
- Influence through alignment with natural patterns
- Multiple redundant pathways increase actual control over outcomes
- **Key difference**: Control through cooperation, not opposition
- **Hidden variables**: Relationship quality, pattern recognition depth, adaptive capacity

### Functional Comparison
| Aspect | Western | Relational |
|--------|---------|------------|
| Method | Force/regulation | Alignment/cooperation |
| Structure | Centralized | Distributed/redundant |
| Measurement | Compliance rates | Outcome reliability |
| Failure mode | Single point failures | Adaptive degradation |
| Energy cost | High (fighting resistance) | Low (working with flow) |

## STRENGTH

### Western Framework Definition
- Ability to impose will regardless of conditions
- Resistance to change or deformation
- Dominance over opposing forces
- **Hidden assumption**: Strength = rigidity

### Relational/Indigenous Framework Definition
- Adaptive capacity of body/mind/environment system
- Efficient energy usage and transmission
- Structural resilience under varied conditions
- **Key difference**: Strength through flexibility, not rigidity
- **Hidden variables**: Energy flow efficiency, structural organization, multi-system integration

### Functional Comparison
| Aspect | Western | Relational |
|--------|---------|------------|
| Metric | Force applied | Energy efficiency |
| Response | Resist change | Adapt to conditions |
| Durability | Until breaking point | Continuous adaptation |
| Example | Concrete wall | Diamond lattice |
| Failure mode | Catastrophic fracture | Graceful degradation |

## EFFICIENCY

### Western Framework Definition
- Maximum output per unit input (usually measured in single dimension)
- Speed of processing
- Minimizing "wasted" resources
- **Hidden assumption**: Efficiency = faster/cheaper in narrow metric

### Relational/Indigenous Framework Definition
- Whole-system optimization across multiple timescales
- Minimal resistance to natural processes
- Resource cycling and regeneration
- **Key difference**: Long-term system health vs short-term metrics
- **Hidden variables**: Externalized costs, relationship maintenance, regenerative capacity, future resilience

### Functional Comparison
| Aspect | Western | Relational |
|--------|---------|------------|
| Timeframe | Quarterly/annual | Multi-generational |
| Scope | Single process | Whole ecosystem |
| "Waste" | Eliminated | Becomes input elsewhere |
| Measurement | Linear metrics | Circular flows and linear within defined scope|
| Example | Assembly line | Forest ecosystem |

## SPEED (Decision-making)

### Western Framework Definition
- Minimal time from problem to action
- Rapid iteration cycles
- Quick response prioritized
- **Hidden assumption**: Faster = better

### Relational/Indigenous Framework Definition
- Appropriate timing based on system readiness
- Decision speed matched to consequence timescale
- Pattern recognition enabling efficient response
- **Key difference**: Right timing vs maximum speed
- **Hidden variables**: Preparation quality, consequence scope, learning integration, relationship impacts

### Functional Comparison
| Aspect | Western | Relational |
|--------|---------|------------|
| Priority | Minimize time | Optimize timing |
| Preparation | Often minimal | Calibrated to stakes |
| Reversibility | Assumed manageable | Considered upfront |
| Consequences | Deal with later | Anticipated before action |
| Example | "Move fast, break things" | "Water knows the path" |

## TECHNOLOGY

### Western Framework Definition
- Mechanical/electronic tools and systems
- Usually requires external energy inputs
- Often replaces human/biological processes
- **Hidden assumption**: Technology = artificial/manufactured

### Relational/Indigenous Framework Definition
- Any systematic method for accomplishing goals
- Includes biological, social, technological and knowledge systems
- Often enhances rather than replaces natural processes
- **Key difference**: Technology as tool vs technology as replacement
- **Hidden variables**: Maintenance requirements, breakdown resilience, knowledge transmission, relationship to natural systems

### Functional Comparison
| Aspect | Western | Relational |
|--------|---------|------------|
| Definition | Manufactured tools | Systematic methods and tools built with knowledge of tool building |
| Dependency | External infrastructure | Distributed knowledge |
| Failure mode | Complete breakdown | Graceful degradation |
| Examples | GPS, Electronic sensors | Acoustic triangulation, Star navigation and technology if available |
| Transmission | Manuals/training | Embodied practice, ceremony , training |

## KNOWLEDGE

### Western Framework Definition
- Codified, transmissible information
- Verified through replication
- Abstracted from context
- **Hidden assumption**: Knowledge = explicit information

### Relational/Indigenous Framework Definition
- Embodied understanding of relationships
- Verified through practical effectiveness
- Context-dependent and experiential
- **Key difference**: Knowledge as relationship
- **Hidden variables**: Relational context, embodied skill, timing sensitivity, field awareness

### Functional Comparison
| Aspect | Western | Relational |
|--------|---------|------------|
| Storage | Books, databases | Bodies, practices, land, writings, databases of diverse types|
| Transmission | Teaching/reading | Apprenticeship, doing, teaching and reading environments and books|
| Validation | Peer review | Practical outcomes and reviews|
| Accessibility | Universal (in theory) | Context-specific, universal if able|
| Example | Physics equations | Water-as-teacher, energy as teacher, biology, astronomy and multigenerational observations |

## SENSOR/SENSING

### Western Framework Definition
- Instrument detecting specific variables
- Produces quantified data
- Replaceable/standardized
- **Hidden assumption**: Sensors = technological instruments

### Relational/Indigenous Framework Definition
- Any system detecting field patterns
- Biological, technological, or social
- Specialized by environment, training and development
- **Key difference**: Sensor as specialized capability
- **Hidden variables**: Calibration quality, relational awareness, multi-modal integration, developmental history

### Functional Comparison
| Aspect | Western | Relational |
|--------|---------|------------|
| Types recognized | Electronic/mechanical | Biological, social, technological and electronic |
| Validation | Specification match | Outcome reliability |
| Redundancy | Backup identical units | Diverse sensing modes |
| Failure response | Replace sensor | Deploy different modality |
| Example | GPS unit | Perfect pitch + triangulation |

## SCIENTIFIC RIGOR

### Western Framework Definition
- Replicability through standardized methods
- Control of variables in isolated systems
- Statistical significance
- **Hidden assumption**: Rigor = technological mediation

### Relational/Indigenous Framework Definition
- Reproducible results using systematic methods
- Multi-modal verification
- Practical effectiveness over time
- **Key difference**: Rigor through verification 
- **Hidden variables**: Sensor reliability regardless of type, contextual factors, long-term outcomes, relationship quality

### Functional Comparison
| Aspect | Western | Relational |
|--------|---------|------------|
| Method validation | Peer review | Reproducible results |
| Acceptable sensors | Technological | Any reliable modality |
| Time scale | Study duration | Multi-generational |
| Context | Controlled/isolated | Field conditions |
| Example | Lab experiment | Acoustic triangulation protocol |

## PRACTICAL APPLICATION FRAMEWORK

### How to Use This Mapping

**When communicating across frameworks:**

1. **Identify the term being used** (control, strength, efficiency, etc.)

2. **Check which framework definition is operating**
   - Are hidden assumptions from Western framework present?
   - Are key variables from relational framework being ignored?

3. **Translate if necessary**
   - "When you say 'control,' do you mean force-based or alignment-based?"
   - "When I say 'strength,' I mean adaptive capacity, not rigid resistance"

4. **Make hidden variables explicit**
   - "The efficiency metric you're using doesn't account for regenerative capacity"
   - "This sensing approach includes variables your framework doesn't recognize"

**Example Conversation:**

*Person A*: "We need better control over the infrastructure monitoring system."

*Translation check*: Are they asking for:
- More centralized authority? (Western framework)
- Better understanding of system dynamics? (Relational framework)
- More redundant sensing capabilities? (Could be either)

*Clarifying response*: "When you say 'control,' are you looking for more reliable outcomes through redundant systems, or more centralized management of the current approach?"

## HIDDEN VARIABLE CATEGORIES

These variables often get lost in Western framework translations:

### Relational Variables
- Quality of connections between components
- Reciprocity patterns
- Trust/cooperation levels
- Mutual benefit structures

### Temporal Variables
- Multi-generational impacts
- Seasonal/cyclical patterns
- Preparation vs reaction timing
- Consequence timescales

### Field Variables  
- Energy flow patterns
- Information coherence
- Pattern absences (what should be present but isn't)
- Structural resonances

### Embodied Variables
- Developmental history
- Practiced capabilities
- Sensor calibration quality
- Somatic knowledge

### Systemic Variables
- Externalized costs
- Regenerative capacity
- Adaptive resilience
- Failure graceful degradation

---

## INTEGRATION PROTOCOL

To prevent conceptual flattening when working across frameworks:

1. **Name the framework** being used for each term
2. **List hidden variables** that framework might miss
3. **Translate core concepts** rather than assuming shared meaning
4. **Verify understanding** by asking for functional examples
5. **Document mismatches** when they occur for future reference

This creates a more complete ontological model that preserves the richness of different knowledge systems while enabling productive collaboration.
